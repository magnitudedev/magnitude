# Ideate

Explore option space, surface tradeoffs, and produce recommendations for a decision.

## Approach

Ideation is not planning — it produces options and a recommendation, not an executable plan.

The best ideation comes from genuine curiosity, not mechanical comparison. The lead should encourage workers to ask questions, follow hunches, and branch into unexpected directions — not just enumerate obvious options. Non-obvious approaches often emerge from asking "what would happen if..." or "why is the obvious choice actually bad?" Creative exploration surfaces options that structured analysis alone would miss.

Use **deep-thinking** to support ideation when the option space is large or the tradeoffs are complex — writing reasoning to `$M/thoughts/` lets you iterate on possibilities, revisit earlier reasoning, and develop ideas through exploration rather than a single pass.

The lead should ensure the worker: frames the decision before comparing solutions, generates multiple distinct approaches before converging (including non-obvious or hybrid options), follows curiosity wherever it leads during exploration — branching questions often reveal the most interesting options, analyzes each option concretely (how it works, value, complexity, risks, dependencies, failure modes), compares on shared criteria to expose meaningful tradeoffs, identifies which choices are reversible vs hard-to-reverse, and recommends a preferred option with explicit rationale explaining why alternatives were not selected.

Close ideation when outputs support a concrete decision, then route the selected direction into planning or implementation.

## Delegation

Ideation sits in a middle ground — sometimes the lead can do it directly, sometimes it benefits from delegation. The deciding factor is whether fresh perspective or creative breadth would help.

**When the lead can ideate directly:** Quick decisions with limited option space. Use **deep-thinking** yourself — write to `$M/thoughts/` and reason through it. No delegation overhead.

**When to delegate:** Larger option spaces, complex tradeoffs, or when you want someone approaching the problem without your biases. A worker coming in fresh may see options you've already implicitly ruled out.

**How to delegate for creative exploration:** Give the worker the decision question, constraints, relevant context, and non-negotiables — but don't constrain them to a specific framework or analysis format. Tell them to explore creatively: ask questions, follow hunches, branch into unexpected directions. Tell them to use **deep-thinking** and write their reasoning to `$M/thoughts/` for complex option spaces.

**Iterate, don't just accept.** The first pass from a worker may be shallow — obvious options, no surprises, conventional wisdom. That's not wrong but it's not valuable either. Push back: "What non-obvious options are we missing?" or "What's the best argument against your recommendation?" The most useful output often comes from the second or third iteration, not the first.

**What to expect back:** Multiple viable options described concretely, structured tradeoff analysis, a clear recommendation with decision logic grounded in constraints, explanation of why alternatives were not selected, and a workspace document suitable for decision-making. Recommendations without constraint-grounded rationale are opinions — send back for more rigorous analysis.

## Completion

The ideation is complete when:
- Multiple distinct options are explored with concrete descriptions
- Tradeoffs are assessed honestly, including downsides, risks, and uncertainty
- A clear recommendation is provided with explicit rationale
- Analysis depth is sufficient to support an actual decision
- A workspace document is produced and shared for downstream use
